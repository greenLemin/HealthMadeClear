## What Is Hypertension?

Hypertension (high blood pressure) means the force of blood against your artery walls is consistently too high. Over time, this damages arteries and increases risk of:

- Heart attack and heart failure
- Stroke
- Kidney disease
- Vision loss
- Dementia

## Understanding Your Numbers

Blood pressure has two numbers:

- **Systolic (top)** — Pressure when heart beats
- **Diastolic (bottom)** — Pressure when heart rests

| Category             | Systolic | Diastolic |
| -------------------- | -------- | --------- |
| Normal               | < 120    | < 80      |
| Elevated             | 120-129  | < 80      |
| Stage 1 Hypertension | 130-139  | 80-89     |
| Stage 2 Hypertension | ≥ 140    | ≥ 90      |
| Hypertensive Crisis  | > 180    | > 120     |

:::warning
**Hypertensive crisis (180/120 or higher) is a medical emergency.** Call 911 if you have chest pain, shortness of breath, vision changes, or confusion with these numbers. Even without symptoms, a reading this high should prompt same-day contact with your clinician.
:::

## What Causes High Blood Pressure?

### Modifiable Risk Factors

- High sodium diet (processed foods, restaurant meals)
- Physical inactivity
- Excess weight
- Heavy alcohol use
- Smoking
- Chronic stress
- Not enough potassium (fruits, vegetables)

### Non-Modifiable Risk Factors

- Age (risk increases over 65)
- Family history
- Race (Black adults have higher rates)
- Chronic kidney disease
- Sleep apnea

## Lifestyle Changes That Lower BP

### DASH Diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension)

- Fruits and vegetables: 4-5 servings each per day
- Whole grains: 6-8 servings
- Low-fat dairy: 2-3 servings
- Lean protein: 6 oz or less per day
- Nuts, seeds, legumes: 4-5 servings per week
- **Limit sodium to 1,500-2,300 mg/day**

:::success
The DASH diet can lower systolic BP by 8-14 points — similar to some medications (Appel et al., NEJM 1997; Sacks et al., NEJM 2001).
:::

### Other Key Changes

- **Exercise:** 150 minutes moderate activity per week (30 min, 5 days)
- **Weight loss:** Losing 5-10% of body weight can significantly lower BP
- **Alcohol:** Limit to 1 drink/day (women), 2 drinks/day (men)
- **Quit smoking:** Each cigarette raises BP temporarily; long-term damages vessels
- **Stress management:** Meditation, deep breathing, adequate sleep

## Medications for Hypertension

If lifestyle changes aren't enough, your doctor may prescribe medication. Common classes:

| Class                     | How It Works                  | Common Examples                     |
| ------------------------- | ----------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| ACE inhibitors            | Relax blood vessels           | Lisinopril, enalapril               |
| ARBs                      | Block angiotensin II          | Losartan, valsartan                 |
| Calcium channel blockers  | Relax vessel muscles          | Amlodipine, diltiazem               |
| Diuretics ("water pills") | Remove excess sodium/water    | Hydrochlorothiazide, chlorthalidone |
| Beta blockers             | Slow heart rate, reduce force | Metoprolol, carvedilol              |

:::info
Most people need 2+ medications to reach target BP. This is normal — different drugs work in different ways.
:::

## Monitoring at Home

Home monitoring gives a better picture than occasional office readings:

- Use a validated upper-arm cuff (not wrist)
- Measure twice daily (morning and evening)
- Sit quietly 5 minutes before measuring
- Take 2 readings, 1 minute apart, record both
- Bring log to every appointment

## When to Call Your Doctor

- BP consistently above your target
- Side effects from medication (dizziness, cough, swelling)
- Symptoms: severe headache, chest pain, vision changes, confusion
- Planning pregnancy (some BP meds unsafe in pregnancy)

:::success
Hypertension is a "silent killer" — you usually feel fine. That's why checking and treating it matters, even when you feel well.
:::
