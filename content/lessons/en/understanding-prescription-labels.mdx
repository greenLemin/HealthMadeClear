## What is a Prescription Label?

A prescription label is the sticker on your medicine bottle that tells you how to take your medicine safely. It contains important information about your medication, including the dosage, when to take it, and any special instructions.

## Key Parts of a Label

Every prescription label has several important sections:

1. Your name - to make sure the medicine is for you
2. Drug name and strength - what the medicine is and how strong it is
3. Dosage - how much to take
4. Frequency - how often to take it
5. Special instructions - any warnings or special directions
6. Expiration date - when the medicine should no longer be used

:::info
Always check that your name is on the label before taking any medication.
:::

## Understanding Dosage Instructions

Dosage instructions tell you exactly how much medicine to take and when. Common instructions include:

- "Take one tablet daily" means take one pill every day at the same time
- "Take with food" means eat something before taking the medicine
- "Take on an empty stomach" means wait 2 hours after eating or 1 hour before eating
- "Twice daily" means take it two times per day, usually morning and evening

:::warning
Never take more than the prescribed amount. If you miss a dose, ask your pharmacist what to do.
:::

## Special Warnings

Prescription labels may include special warnings such as:

- "May cause drowsiness" - the medicine might make you sleepy, so don't drive
- "Avoid alcohol" - drinking alcohol while taking this medicine can be dangerous
- "Store in a cool, dry place" - keep the medicine away from heat and moisture
- "Shake well before use" - for liquid medicines, mix it before pouring

## Questions to Ask Your Pharmacist

If anything on your label is unclear, ask your pharmacist:

1. What is this medicine for?
2. How and when should I take it?
3. What should I do if I miss a dose?
4. Are there any side effects I should know about?
5. Can I take this with my other medicines?

:::success
Your pharmacist is there to help. Never hesitate to ask questions about your medications.
:::

## Refills and Pharmacy Information

The label also shows how many refills remain and the prescriber's name. When refills run out, contact your clinic — do not skip doses while waiting. The pharmacy phone number on the label is the fastest way to clarify label instructions.

## Storing Medicine at Home

Keep medicines in their original containers so labels stay readable. Store away from heat, moisture, and direct sunlight unless the label says refrigeration is required. Keep medicines out of reach of children and pets, and dispose of expired drugs through a pharmacy take-back program when possible.
