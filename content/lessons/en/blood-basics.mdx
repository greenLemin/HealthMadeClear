## Why Blood Tests?

Blood tests help doctors see what's happening inside your body without surgery. A small sample can reveal:

- How well organs like kidneys and liver are working
- Signs of infection, inflammation, or anemia
- Diabetes or prediabetes
- Cholesterol and heart disease risk
- Thyroid, hormone, and vitamin levels
- How well medications are working
- Whether treatment is improving a condition

Blood tests are often part of routine checkups, pre-surgery screening, or monitoring chronic illness.

## What's in Your Blood?

Blood has four main components:

- **Red blood cells** — carry oxygen using hemoglobin
- **White blood cells** — fight infection
- **Platelets** — help blood clot
- **Plasma** — liquid that carries cells, nutrients, and waste

Different tests measure different parts. Your doctor orders tests based on your symptoms and health history.

## Common Blood Tests

Some of the most common panels include:

- **Complete Blood Count (CBC)** — red cells, white cells, platelets, hemoglobin, hematocrit
- **Basic Metabolic Panel (BMP)** — kidney function (creatinine), electrolytes, blood sugar
- **Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP)** — BMP plus liver enzymes
- **Lipid Panel** — total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, triglycerides
- **Hemoglobin A1C** — average blood sugar over 2–3 months
- **Thyroid panel** — TSH and sometimes T4/T3

You don't need every test at every visit. More isn't always better — targeted testing is the goal.

## Before Your Blood Draw

Ask your clinic:

- Do I need to fast? (Often 8–12 hours for glucose and lipids)
- Can I take my regular medicines?
- Should I drink water beforehand? (Usually yes — it makes veins easier to find)

During the draw, tell the phlebotomist if you've fainted before or have needle anxiety.

## Understanding Reference Ranges

Results compare your numbers to a **reference range** — values typical for most healthy people in that lab. Your result may be:

- **Within range** — often normal, but context matters
- **High** — above the upper limit
- **Low** — below the lower limit

Being slightly outside the range isn't always a problem. Pregnancy, medicines, dehydration, and recent illness can shift numbers. Your doctor interprets results with your full history — not in isolation.

:::info
Reference ranges vary by lab, age, sex, and equipment. Always ask your doctor to explain what your numbers mean for you.
:::

:::success
Request a copy of your results through your patient portal. Tracking trends over time is often more useful than a single number.
:::
