## What Is Asthma?

Asthma is a chronic lung disease where airways become inflamed, narrow, and produce extra mucus. This makes breathing difficult. Symptoms include:

- Wheezing (whistling sound when breathing)
- Shortness of breath
- Chest tightness
- Coughing (especially at night or early morning)

## What Happens During an Asthma Attack?

1. **Trigger** irritates airways
2. **Inflammation** — airway lining swells
3. **Bronchoconstriction** — muscles around airways tighten
4. **Mucus** — thick mucus clogs airways
5. **Result** — air can't move in and out easily

:::warning
**Severe asthma attack is a medical emergency.** Call 911 if: rescue inhaler doesn't help, can't speak in full sentences, lips/fingernails blue, chest/neck skin pulls in with breathing.
:::

## Common Triggers

| Category    | Examples                                                               |
| ----------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Allergens   | Pollen, dust mites, mold, pet dander, cockroaches                      |
| Irritants   | Smoke (tobacco, wood, wildfire), strong odors, air pollution, cold air |
| Exercise    | Especially in cold, dry air                                            |
| Infections  | Colds, flu, COVID-19, sinus infections                                 |
| Emotions    | Strong laughter, crying, stress                                        |
| Medications | Aspirin, NSAIDs (ibuprofen), beta blockers (in some people)            |

## Two Types of Inhalers

### 1. Quick-Relief (Rescue) Inhaler

- **Short-acting beta agonist (SABA):** Albuterol, levalbuterol
- **Use:** For sudden symptoms or before exercise (if prescribed)
- **Works in:** Minutes
- **Lasts:** 4-6 hours
- **Color code:** Often **red** or blue

:::info
If you use your rescue inhaler more than 2 times per week (not counting pre-exercise), your asthma is not well controlled. Talk to your doctor.
:::

### 2. Controller (Maintenance) Inhaler

- **Inhaled corticosteroids (ICS):** Fluticasone, budesonide, beclomethasone
- **Use:** Daily, even when feeling well
- **Works over:** Days to weeks
- **Prevents:** Inflammation and attacks
- **Color code:** Often **orange**, brown, or purple

### Combination Inhalers

- **ICS + Long-acting beta agonist (LABA):** Fluticasone/salmeterol, budesonide/formoterol
- **Used as:** Daily controller (some also approved for rescue)

## How to Use a Metered-Dose Inhaler (MDI) with Spacer

1. **Shake** the inhaler well (5-10 seconds)
2. **Attach** spacer to inhaler
3. **Breathe out** fully, away from spacer
4. **Place** spacer mouthpiece in mouth, seal lips
5. **Press** inhaler **once**
6. **Breathe in** slowly and deeply (3-5 seconds)
7. **Hold breath** 10 seconds (or as long as comfortable)
8. **Breathe out** slowly
9. **Wait** 1 minute between puffs if 2 puffs prescribed
10. **Rinse mouth** with water after steroid inhalers

:::success
A spacer (holding chamber) doubles the amount of medicine reaching your lungs. Always use one with MDIs.
:::

## Asthma Action Plan

A written plan from your doctor with three zones:

### Green Zone (Doing Well)

- No symptoms
- Peak flow 80-100% of personal best
- **Action:** Take daily controller meds as prescribed

### Yellow Zone (Caution)

- Symptoms: cough, wheeze, chest tight, night waking
- Peak flow 50-79% of personal best
- **Action:** Add quick-relief inhaler (2-4 puffs every 20 min up to 1 hour). Call doctor if not improving.

### Red Zone (Emergency)

- Severe symptoms: can't talk, rescue inhaler not helping
- Peak flow < 50% of personal best
- **Action:** Take quick-relief inhaler (4-6 puffs). **Call 911 or go to ER.**

## Managing Asthma Daily

- **Take controller meds daily** — even when feeling fine
- **Identify and avoid triggers** — allergy testing can help
- **Monitor peak flow** — if your doctor recommends it
- **Stay active** — exercise is good for asthma; use pre-exercise inhaler if prescribed
- **Get vaccinated** — flu, COVID-19, pneumonia vaccines
- **Quit smoking** — smoke is a major trigger
- **Treat allergies** — nasal sprays, antihistamines help

## When to See Your Doctor

- Symptoms more than 2 days/week
- Night waking more than 2 times/month
- Rescue inhaler use more than 2 days/week
- Limiting activities due to asthma
- Peak flow in yellow or red zone
- Need to refill rescue inhaler more than twice/year
