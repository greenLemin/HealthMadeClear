## Start With Your Main Concern

When the doctor asks "What brings you in today?" lead with your most important concern. Use the symptom notes you prepared. A clear opening saves time and helps the doctor prioritize.

Example: "I've had chest tightness when I climb stairs for about two weeks. It's new for me and I'm worried."

## Be Honest and Complete

Tell your doctor everything about your health, even if it feels embarrassing. Your doctor needs the full picture to help you. Don't leave anything out, including:

- All medications you take (including supplements and herbal products)
- How closely you follow your medication schedule
- Alcohol, tobacco, or substance use
- Your diet, sleep, and exercise habits
- Mental health concerns (stress, anxiety, mood changes)
- Any changes in your health since your last visit
- Your concerns and fears

Doctors hear sensitive topics every day. Honesty leads to better care.

## Ask for Clarification

Medical language can be confusing. If your doctor uses terms you don't understand, ask them to explain in plain language. You can say:

- "Can you explain that in simpler terms?"
- "What does that word mean?"
- "Can you write that down for me?"
- "Can you give me an example?"
- "What would that look like in my daily life?"

There are no bad questions. Understanding your care plan helps you follow it correctly.

## Take Notes

Write down important information during your visit, including:

- Your diagnosis (what's wrong, in words you understand)
- Your treatment plan and why it was chosen
- Medication names, doses, and timing
- Tests ordered and when results will be ready
- When to schedule follow-up appointments
- Warning signs that mean you should call or seek emergency care

If writing is hard during the visit, ask to record a voice memo or request a written summary.

## Understand Your Next Steps

Before you leave, make sure you know:

- What you should do today (pick up prescriptions, schedule tests)
- What you should do this week (start a new medicine, rest, change diet)
- Who to call with questions (nurse line, pharmacy, specialist)
- How to access test results (patient portal, phone call)

Repeat back the plan in your own words: "So I'll start the antibiotic tonight, finish the full course, and call if I get a rash — is that right?"

## Advocate for Yourself

You have the right to:

- Ask for a second opinion
- Request a different treatment if side effects are severe
- Bring a trusted person into the exam room
- Ask for an interpreter if English is not your first language

:::success
It's okay to ask your doctor to slow down or repeat something you missed. Good care is a partnership.
:::

:::warning
If you feel unsafe, dismissed, or confused about urgent symptoms, speak up. Say: "I'm still worried about this. Can we talk about what else could be causing it?"
:::
