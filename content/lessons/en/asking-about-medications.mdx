## Why Ask Questions?

Asking questions about your medications helps you understand what you're taking and why. It can prevent mistakes, side effects, and dangerous drug interactions. You have the right to know everything about your medicines — and pharmacists are trained to answer medication questions even after you leave the doctor's office.

Studies show that many people don't take medicines as prescribed because they don't understand instructions or fear side effects. Questions reduce those barriers.

## Essential Questions for Your Doctor

When your doctor prescribes a new medication, ask:

1. **What is the name of the medicine?** (brand and generic)
2. **What is it supposed to do?** How will I know it's working?
3. **How and when should I take it?** With food? At bedtime?
4. **How long will I need to take it?** Days, months, or ongoing?
5. **What side effects should I watch for?** Which ones need a call to you?
6. **Are there foods, drinks, or activities I should avoid?**
7. **What should I do if I miss a dose?**
8. **Will this interact with my other medicines or supplements?**
9. **Is there a less expensive generic version?**
10. **How should I store this medicine?**

Write answers on your medication list or in your phone.

## Questions for Your Pharmacist

Pharmacists are medication experts. When you pick up a prescription, ask:

- Can you review all my medicines for interactions?
- What should I do if I experience side effects?
- Does this need to be taken with food?
- Can I crush or split this pill if swallowing is hard?
- What is the best way to remember doses?

Bring your full medication list every time — including vitamins and herbal products.

## About Drug Interactions

Drug interactions happen when two or more substances affect each other. They can make a drug stronger, weaker, or cause new side effects. Always tell your doctor and pharmacist about:

- All prescription medicines
- Over-the-counter medicines (aspirin, ibuprofen, cold medicine)
- Vitamins and supplements
- Herbal remedies (St. John's wort, ginseng, etc.)
- Recreational substances
- Any drug allergies or past bad reactions

Even common combinations — like blood thinners plus aspirin — can be risky without medical guidance.

## Keep an Updated Medication List

Your list should include:

- Drug name (brand and generic)
- Dose and how often you take it
- Who prescribed it and why
- Pharmacy phone number

Update the list whenever something changes. Share it at every medical visit, urgent care trip, and hospital stay.

:::warning
Keep a list of all your medicines and show it to every doctor you visit. "I take a little white pill" is not enough — names and doses matter.
:::

:::info
Pill organizers, phone alarms, and pharmacy refill reminders help you stay on schedule. Ask your pharmacist which tools fit your routine.
:::
