## What Are Side Effects?

Side effects are unwanted reactions to a medication. They can range from mild (dry mouth) to severe (allergic reaction). Not everyone experiences side effects, and many fade as your body adjusts — especially during the first one to two weeks.

Side effects are different from the medicine not working. If symptoms don't improve after the expected time, tell your doctor.

## Common Mild Side Effects

These are usually not emergencies and may improve on their own or with simple strategies:

- Nausea or upset stomach — try taking with food unless told otherwise
- Drowsiness or dizziness — avoid driving until you know how you react
- Dry mouth — sip water, use sugar-free gum
- Headache — rest, hydrate; call if severe or persistent
- Mild rash or itching — monitor; report if spreading
- Constipation or diarrhea — diet changes may help; ask pharmacist

If mild effects bother your daily life, don't stop the medicine on your own. Call your doctor or pharmacist first. They may adjust the dose, change timing, or switch to another drug.

## Strategies That Help

- Take medicine exactly as prescribed — timing matters
- Don't skip doses to "avoid" side effects
- Keep a symptom log (what you felt, when, severity)
- Ask whether side effects are temporary
- Never combine medicines without checking interactions

## When to Call Your Doctor

Call your doctor promptly (same day) if you have:

- Side effects that interfere with eating, sleeping, or working
- Persistent vomiting or diarrhea
- Confusion or unusual mood changes
- Muscle pain with dark urine (possible muscle injury from some statins)
- Worsening rash that doesn't fade

## When to Seek Emergency Care

Call 911 or go to the emergency room if you experience:

- Trouble breathing or swallowing
- Severe rash, hives, or swelling of face, lips, or tongue
- Severe dizziness, fainting, or chest pain
- Rapid or irregular heartbeat
- Severe stomach pain or vomiting blood
- Yellowing of skin or eyes
- Sudden weakness on one side, slurred speech, or confusion (stroke signs)
- Unusual bleeding or bruising

:::warning
If you think you're having a serious allergic reaction or other emergency, call 911. Do not wait to see if it passes.
:::

## Stopping Medicine Safely

Never stop a prescription suddenly unless your doctor says so. Some medicines (steroids, certain heart drugs, antidepressants) need gradual tapering to avoid withdrawal or rebound illness.

:::info
Report side effects to the FDA MedWatch program (fda.gov/safety/medwatch). Your report helps identify safety issues for everyone.
:::
