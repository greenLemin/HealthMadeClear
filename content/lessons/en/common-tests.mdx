## How to Read This Lesson

Lab results can feel overwhelming. This lesson explains common tests in plain language. **Only your doctor can diagnose you** — use this as background for better conversations, not self-diagnosis.

## Complete Blood Count (CBC)

A CBC measures several parts of your blood:

- **Red blood cells (RBC)** — carry oxygen; low counts may cause fatigue (anemia)
- **White blood cells (WBC)** — fight infection; high may suggest infection or inflammation
- **Platelets** — help clotting; low increases bleeding risk
- **Hemoglobin** — oxygen-carrying protein in red cells
- **Hematocrit** — percentage of blood volume that is red cells

Doctors use CBC to screen for anemia, infection, and some blood disorders.

## Cholesterol and Lipid Panel

Cholesterol tests measure fats in your blood:

- **Total cholesterol** — sum of cholesterol types
- **LDL ("bad") cholesterol** — can build up in artery walls
- **HDL ("good") cholesterol** — helps remove LDL
- **Triglycerides** — stored energy from food; high levels raise heart risk

:::warning
High LDL and low HDL increase heart disease and stroke risk over time. Lifestyle and sometimes medicine can improve these numbers.
:::

Target levels depend on your age, diabetes status, and overall heart risk. Ask your doctor what targets apply to you.

## Blood Sugar Tests

Blood sugar tests screen for diabetes and prediabetes:

- **Fasting glucose** — blood sugar after not eating 8+ hours
- **Hemoglobin A1C** — average blood sugar over 2–3 months; no fasting needed
- **Oral glucose tolerance test** — how your body handles sugar after a sweet drink

Prediabetes often has no symptoms. Early detection allows lifestyle changes that may prevent diabetes.

## Kidney Function (BMP/CMP)

**Creatinine** and **BUN** (blood urea nitrogen) reflect how well kidneys filter waste. High levels may indicate kidney stress from dehydration, medicines, or chronic kidney disease.

**Electrolytes** (sodium, potassium, chloride) balance fluids and nerves. Abnormal levels need medical attention — especially potassium.

## Liver Enzymes

**ALT** and **AST** rise when liver cells are injured. Causes range from fatty liver disease and alcohol use to medicines and viral hepatitis. Mild elevations are common; your doctor looks at the pattern and your symptoms.

## Thyroid Tests

**TSH** (thyroid-stimulating hormone) is the main screening test. Abnormal TSH may lead to testing of **T4** and **T3**. Thyroid problems can affect energy, weight, mood, and heart rate.

## Questions to Ask About Your Results

- Is this result normal for me?
- Should we repeat the test?
- Do I need treatment or lifestyle changes?
- How does this connect to my symptoms or medicines?

:::info
Save your lab reports. Comparing this year's cholesterol to last year's often matters more than a single number in isolation.
:::
