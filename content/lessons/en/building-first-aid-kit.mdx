## Choosing Your Kit Container

- **Plastic storage bin** with lid — waterproof, easy to carry
- **Clear** — you can see contents without opening
- **Label clearly** — "FIRST AID" with a red cross
- **Size:** Big enough for supplies, small enough to carry

## Essential Supplies

### Wound Care

- **Adhesive bandages** — assorted sizes (including butterfly strips for small cuts)
- **Sterile gauze pads** — 4x4 inches (pack of 10-20)
- **Medical tape** — cloth tape to secure gauze (paper tape for sensitive skin)
- **Antiseptic wipes** — individually wrapped (alcohol or chlorhexidine)
- **Antibiotic ointment** — bacitracin or triple antibiotic (Neosporin)
- **Hydrocortisone cream** — 1% for itching and allergic reactions
- **Hydrogen peroxide** — for cleaning minor wounds (don't use on deep wounds)

### Tools

- **Scissors** — sturdy, blunt-tipped for cutting tape, gauze, clothing
- **Tweezers** — fine-tipped for splinters, ticks, glass shards
- **Safety pins** — for slings, securing bandages
- **Instant cold pack** — squeeze to activate (no refrigerator needed)
- **Disposable gloves** — nitrile or vinyl (latex allergy risk)
- **CPR face shield** — for giving rescue breaths safely
- **Flashlight** — small LED with extra batteries
- **Thermometer** — digital (no glass mercury)

### Medications

- **Pain/fever relievers:**
  - Acetaminophen (Tylenol) — safe for most people, doesn't upset stomach
  - Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin) — anti-inflammatory, good for sprains
- **Antihistamine** — diphenhydramine (Benadryl) for allergic reactions
- **Anti-diarrheal** — loperamide (Imodium)
- **Antacids** — calcium carbonate (Tums) or famotidine (Pepcid)
- **Oral rehydration salts** — or sports drink powder packets

### Documentation

- **First aid manual** — print a simple guide from Red Cross website
- **Emergency contact numbers:**
  - 911 (emergency)
  - Poison Control: 1-800-222-1222
  - Doctor's office
  - Local urgent care and ER
  - Pharmacy
- **List of allergies and medical conditions** for each family member
- **Insurance cards** (copies)

## How to Use Key Items

### Cleaning a Minor Wound

1. Wash hands (or wear gloves)
2. Clean wound with cool water and mild soap
3. Pat dry with sterile gauze
4. Apply antibiotic ointment
5. Cover with adhesive bandage or gauze + tape
6. Change bandage daily or when wet/dirty

### Treating a Sprain (RICE Method)

- **R**est — stop using injured area
- **I**ce — apply cold pack 20 minutes on, 20 off
- **C**ompression — wrap with elastic bandage (ACE wrap)
- **E**levation — raise above heart level

:::info
A compression bandage should be snug but not tight. If fingers/toes turn blue or tingle, loosen it.
:::

### Splinter or Tick Removal

1. Clean area with antiseptic wipe
2. Use tweezers to grasp as close to skin as possible
3. Pull straight out (don't twist for splinters; do twist gently for ticks)
4. Clean again and apply antibiotic ointment

## What NOT to Put in Your Kit

- **Tourniquets** (unless trained) — can cause more harm
- **Aspirin for children** — Reye's syndrome risk
- **Ipecac syrup** — no longer recommended for poisoning; call Poison Control instead
- **Opened supplies** — once opened, not sterile
- **Expired medications** — check dates every 6 months

## Seasonal and Special Items

### Summer

- Aloe vera gel (sunburn)
- Insect repellent (DEET or picaridin)
- Sunscreen SPF 30+
- EpiPen if allergic to stings

### Winter

- Hand warmers
- Extra blanket in car kit
- Ice melt/salt packets in car kit

### For Babies and Children

- Children's acetaminophen/ibuprofen (weight-based dose)
- Oral syringe or dosing cup
- Baby-safe thermometer (rectal for infants)
- Orajel or teething gel
- Children's Benadryl

### For Older Adults

- Blood pressure monitor (if hypertensive)
- Glucose tablets (if diabetic)
- Daily medication pill organizer
- Copy of medical power of attorney / advance directive

:::warning
If someone is unconscious, not breathing, severely bleeding, or has a suspected spinal injury, **do not move them** — call 911 immediately.
:::

## When to Restock

- **Every 6 months:** Check expiration dates on all supplies
- **After every use:** Replace used items within 24 hours
- **Time change (spring/fall):** Good reminder to check your kit
- **After a kit is opened:** Replace sterile items

:::success
Your first-aid kit is only useful if you know where it is, what's in it, and how to use it. Review contents yearly with everyone in your home.
:::
