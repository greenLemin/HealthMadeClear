## Key Terms You Need to Know

### Premium

The amount you pay every month for health insurance. You pay this even if you don't use any healthcare.

### Deductible

The amount you pay for covered services before your insurance starts paying. For example: if deductible is $1,500, you pay the first $1,500 of covered care (except free preventive services).

### Copay

A fixed dollar amount you pay at the time of service ($20-$50 per doctor visit, $10 for generic drugs).

### Coinsurance

A percentage you pay after meeting your deductible (e.g., 20% of a bill). If your coinsurance is 20%, you pay 20% and insurance pays 80%.

### Out-of-Pocket Maximum

The most you pay in a year. Once reached, insurance pays 100% of covered services. Includes deductible + copays + coinsurance.

| Term              | You Pay            | Example     |
| ----------------- | ------------------ | ----------- |
| Premium           | Monthly            | $450/month  |
| Deductible        | First $X of care   | $1,500/year |
| Copay             | Per visit          | $30/visit   |
| Coinsurance       | % after deductible | 20%         |
| Out-of-pocket max | Cap per year       | $6,000/year |

## Understanding Networks

- **In-network:** Providers and hospitals that have a contract with your insurance. Lower costs.
- **Out-of-network:** No contract. You pay more — possibly much more.
- **HMO:** You pick a primary care doctor (PCP). They refer you to specialists. Out-of-network not covered (except emergencies).
- **PPO:** See any provider, in or out of network. More flexibility, higher premiums.
- **EPO:** In-network only (like HMO) but can see specialists without referral (like PPO).

:::warning
**Out-of-network care can be extremely expensive.** Always check if a doctor or hospital is in your network before making an appointment. Emergency care is usually covered at in-network rates.
:::

## Types of Plans

### Employer-Sponsored Insurance

- Most common (49% of Americans)
- Employer typically pays ~70% of premium
- Enroll during open enrollment or after qualifying life event

### Marketplace (ACA / Obamacare)

- Healthcare.gov or state equivalent
- Income-based subsidies (premium tax credits) available
- Open enrollment: Nov 1 - Jan 15 (varies by state)
- Four tiers: Bronze (lowest premium, highest costs) → Platinum (highest premium, lowest costs)

| Metal Tier | Plan Pays | You Pay (avg) | Best For                             |
| ---------- | --------- | ------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Bronze     | 60%       | 40%           | Young, healthy, few visits           |
| Silver     | 70%       | 30%           | Most people; subsidy available       |
| Gold       | 80%       | 20%           | Regular doctor visits, prescriptions |
| Platinum   | 90%       | 10%           | Frequent care, chronic conditions    |

### Medicare

- Federal program for 65+ and certain disabilities
- Part A (hospital) — free if you worked 10+ years
- Part B (medical) — ~$175/month (2026)
- Part D (drugs) — varies
- Medigap (supplemental) — covers what Original Medicare doesn't

### Medicaid

- State + federal program for low-income individuals
- Eligibility varies by state (expanded in most states under ACA)
- Low or no cost

## How to Choose a Plan

When comparing plans, ask:

1. **Are my doctors and hospital in-network?**
2. **What drugs are covered?** (Check the formulary)
3. **What's the total estimated cost?** Premium + expected care costs
4. **Do I have chronic conditions?** Higher deductible plans cost more long-term for regular care
5. **What's the out-of-pocket max?** Worst-case scenario

:::info
The cheapest premium isn't always the cheapest plan. If you have regular doctor visits or prescriptions, a Silver or Gold plan may cost less overall than Bronze.
:::

## Using Your Insurance

- **Get an ID card** — carry it in wallet and phone
- **Find in-network providers** — search online directory or call member services
- **Know your copays** — $ for primary care, specialist, urgent care, ER
- **Get pre-authorization** — some tests and surgeries require approval
- **Check for step therapy** — may need to try cheaper drug first
- **Appeal if denied** — you have the right to challenge coverage denials

## What If You Lose or Can't Afford Insurance?

- **COBRA:** Continue employer plan for 18 months (you pay full premium)
- **Marketplace:** Special enrollment period (60 days after losing coverage)
- **Medicaid:** Apply anytime (if eligible in your state)
- **Community health centers:** Sliding-scale fees based on income
- **Hospital financial assistance:** Many hospitals offer charity care

:::success
Understanding your insurance saves money and stress. If terms confuse you, call your insurer's member services line — they must explain benefits clearly.
:::
