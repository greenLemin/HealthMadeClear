## The Updated Nutrition Facts Label

In 2020, the FDA updated the label to make it easier to read. Key changes:

- Serving sizes updated to realistic amounts
- "Added sugars" listed separately
- Larger type for calories
- Vitamin D and potassium added; vitamins A and C removed

## Section 1: Serving Size

**The most important number on the label.** All other numbers are per serving.

- Servings per container: how many servings in the package
- Serving size: amount considered one serving

Example: A bag of chips with 3 servings per container. If you eat the whole bag, multiply everything by 3.

:::info
Serving sizes are standardized by the FDA so you can compare similar products. They're based on what people actually eat, not what they should eat.
:::

## Section 2: Calories

- Calories per serving
- 400+ calories per serving = high (check portion size)

## Section 3: Nutrients to Limit

### Saturated Fat

- **Goal:** Less than 10% of daily calories (about 20g/day on 2000-calorie diet)
- **Found in:** Fatty meats, butter, cheese, full-fat dairy, coconut oil, palm oil
- **Why limit:** Raises LDL ("bad") cholesterol

### Trans Fat

- **Goal:** As little as possible (ideally 0g)
- **Found in:** Partially hydrogenated oils (now banned from US food supply, but still trace amounts)
- **Why limit:** Raises LDL, lowers HDL — no safe amount

### Sodium

- **Goal:** Less than 2,300 mg/day (ideally 1,500 mg)
- **Found in:** Processed foods, restaurant meals, canned soups, breads, deli meats, frozen dinners
- **Why limit:** Raises blood pressure

### Added Sugars

- **Goal:** Less than 50g/day (200 calories, 10% of total)
- **Found in:** Soda, candy, baked goods, flavored yogurt, granola bars, cereals, ketchup, pasta sauce
- **Why limit:** Empty calories linked to obesity, diabetes, heart disease

:::warning
"Added sugars" are different from naturally occurring sugars in fruit and milk. Added sugars are the ones to limit.
:::

## Section 4: Nutrients to Get More Of

### Dietary Fiber

- **Goal:** 25g/day (women), 38g/day (men); Americans average only 15g
- **Found in:** Beans, lentils, vegetables, fruits, whole grains, nuts, seeds
- **Why:** Lowers cholesterol, controls blood sugar, keeps you full

### Vitamin D

- **Goal:** 20mcg (800 IU)/day
- **Found in:** Fatty fish (salmon, tuna), fortified milk, egg yolks
- **Why:** Bone health, immune function; most Americans are low

### Calcium

- **Goal:** 1,000-1,200 mg/day
- **Found in:** Dairy, fortified plant milks, leafy greens, tofu with calcium
- **Why:** Bone density, muscle function, nerve signaling

### Potassium

- **Goal:** 4,700 mg/day
- **Found in:** Bananas, potatoes, beans, leafy greens, avocados, yogurt
- **Why:** Lowers blood pressure, counteracts sodium

### Iron

- **Goal:** 8mg (men), 18mg (women), 27mg (pregnancy)
- **Found in:** Red meat, beans, spinach, fortified cereals
- **Why:** Oxygen transport in blood

## Section 5: The % Daily Value (%DV)

This tells you how much a nutrient contributes to a daily diet (based on 2,000 calories/day).

- **5% DV or less = Low** — use for nutrients to limit (saturated fat, sodium, added sugar)
- **20% DV or more = High** — use for nutrients to get more of (fiber, vitamins, minerals)

## Section 6: Ingredient List

Ingredients are listed in **descending order by weight.** The first ingredient is the most abundant.

- **Watch for:** Sugar under many names (sucrose, high-fructose corn syrup, dextrose, maltose, honey, agave, cane sugar, fruit juice concentrate)
- **Short list = better** — fewer ingredients usually means less processing
- **Look for:** Whole foods as first ingredients (oats, chicken, beans, apples)

## Quick Comparison Example

| Nutrient      | Better Choice | Worse Choice  |
| ------------- | ------------- | ------------- |
| Calories      | 150           | 300           |
| Saturated fat | 1g (5%DV)     | 8g (40%DV)    |
| Sodium        | 140mg (6%DV)  | 700mg (30%DV) |
| Added sugar   | 5g (10%DV)    | 25g (50%DV)   |
| Fiber         | 6g (21%DV)    | 0g (0%DV)     |

## Common Label Tricks

- **"Made with whole grains"** — might be mostly white flour
- **"Natural"** — not regulated; doesn't mean healthy
- **"Low-fat"** — often extra sugar to compensate for taste
- **"No added sugar"** — might still be high in naturally occurring sugar (fruit juice)
- **"Gluten-free"** — healthier if you have celiac disease; not inherently healthier otherwise
- **"Organic"** — fewer pesticides, not fewer calories

:::success
You don't need to read every label perfectly. Focus on the first check: serving size, calories, saturated fat, sodium, added sugar. That catches 90% of the important info.
:::
