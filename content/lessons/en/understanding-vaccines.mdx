## How Vaccines Work

Vaccines train your immune system to recognize and fight specific germs (viruses or bacteria) without making you sick. They contain:

- A weakened or dead germ
- Or just a piece of the germ (like a protein)
- Or instructions (mRNA) for your cells to make a harmless piece

Your immune system learns to recognize the germ and makes antibodies. If you're exposed later, your body fights it off quickly — often before you get sick.

:::info
Think of a vaccine like a "wanted poster" for your immune system. It shows your body what the enemy looks like so it's ready to attack.
:::

## Types of Vaccines

| Type              | How It Works                         | Examples                              |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------- |
| Live attenuated   | Weakened germ                        | MMR, chickenpox, nasal flu            |
| Inactivated       | Killed germ                          | Flu shot, polio, hepatitis A          |
| Subunit/Conjugate | Piece of germ                        | HPV, hepatitis B, shingles (Shingrix) |
| mRNA              | Instructions to make protein         | COVID-19 (Pfizer, Moderna)            |
| Viral vector      | Harmless virus delivers instructions | COVID-19 (J&J)                        |

## Recommended Vaccines for Adults

### Everyone (Annual)

- **Flu (influenza)** — Every fall, protects against that year's strains
- **COVID-19** — As recommended by CDC (updated boosters)

### Every 10 Years

- **Tdap or Td** — Tetanus, diphtheria, pertussis (whooping cough)

### Age 50+

- **Shingles (Shingrix)** — Two doses, 2-6 months apart
- **RSV** — Single dose (ages 60+, talk to doctor)

### Age 65+

- **Pneumococcal** — Protects against pneumonia, meningitis, bloodstream infections
  - PCV20 (one dose) OR
  - PCV15 followed by PPSV23 (one year later)

### Based on Risk Factors

- **Hepatitis A** — Travel, liver disease, men who have sex with men
- **Hepatitis B** — Healthcare workers, diabetes, multiple sex partners
- **HPV** — Through age 26 (or 27-45 after discussion with doctor)
- **Meningococcal** — College dorms, military, certain medical conditions
- **MMR** — If born after 1957 and no evidence of immunity

## Common Myths vs. Facts

| Myth                                   | Fact                                                                                              |
| -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "Vaccines cause autism"                | **False.** Large studies found no link. The original study was retracted.                         |
| "Natural immunity is better"           | **Risky.** Getting the disease can cause severe complications or death.                           |
| "Vaccines contain dangerous toxins"    | **False.** Ingredients are in tiny, safe amounts. Aluminum in vaccines < aluminum in breast milk. |
| "I don't need vaccines if I'm healthy" | **False.** Healthy people can get very sick and spread disease to vulnerable people.              |
| "Vaccines overload the immune system"  | **False.** Your immune system handles thousands of germs daily. Vaccines are a tiny fraction.     |

:::warning
Vaccine side effects (sore arm, low fever, tiredness) are normal and usually last 1-2 days. They mean your immune system is responding. Serious reactions are extremely rare.
:::

## What to Expect When Getting Vaccinated

1. **Before** — Tell the provider about allergies, past reactions, and if you're sick
2. **During** — Quick shot in the upper arm (or nose for nasal flu)
3. **After** — Wait 15 minutes (30 if history of severe allergies)
4. **At home** — Move your arm, stay hydrated, use cool compress if sore

:::success
Keep a vaccine record. Ask your pharmacy or doctor for a copy. Many states have immunization registries.
:::

## Questions to Ask Your Provider

1. Which vaccines do I need right now?
2. Are there any I should avoid due to my health conditions?
3. What side effects are normal?
4. When should I get the next dose?
5. Is there a cost? (Most are covered by insurance)
