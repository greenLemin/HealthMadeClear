## What Is Depression?

Depression (major depressive disorder) is a common medical condition that affects how you think, feel, and handle daily life. It is not sadness or grief — it is a persistent state that lasts at least 2 weeks and interferes with functioning.

## Common Symptoms

- **Emotional:** Persistent sad, empty, or hopeless mood; loss of interest in hobbies; irritability
- **Physical:** Fatigue, sleep changes (too much or too little), significant weight/appetite change, slowed movements
- **Cognitive:** Trouble concentrating, making decisions, or remembering; negative self-talk; excessive guilt
- **Behavioral:** Withdrawing from friends and activities; difficulty doing work or chores

Not everyone experiences all symptoms. Diagnosis requires 5+ symptoms lasting 2+ weeks, with at least one being depressed mood or loss of interest.

## When to Seek Help

Seek professional help if symptoms:

- Last more than 2 weeks
- Interfere with work, school, or relationships
- Cause significant distress
- Include thoughts of self-harm

### Medical emergency — call 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) if:

- Thoughts of suicide
- Plans to harm yourself
- Feeling unable to keep yourself safe

:::warning
If you or someone you know is in crisis, call or text **988** (US). You matter. Help is available 24/7.
:::

## What Causes Depression?

- **Genetics:** Runs in families (but no single "depression gene")
- **Life events:** Trauma, loss, financial stress, divorce
- **Medical conditions:** Chronic pain, thyroid disorders, heart disease, cancer
- **Medications:** Some drugs can trigger depression
- **Brain chemistry:** Imbalance in neurotransmitters (serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine)

## Treatment Options

### Psychotherapy (Talk Therapy)

- **Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)** — Most researched; focuses on changing negative thought patterns
- **Interpersonal Therapy (IPT)** — Focuses on relationships and life changes
- **Duration:** Usually 12-20 sessions over 3-4 months
- **Effectiveness:** 50-70% improvement rate

### Medications (Antidepressants)

| Class     | How It Works                        | Examples                                                         |
| --------- | ----------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| SSRIs     | Increase serotonin                  | Fluoxetine (Prozac), sertraline (Zoloft), escitalopram (Lexapro) |
| SNRIs     | Increase serotonin + norepinephrine | Venlafaxine (Effexor), duloxetine (Cymbalta)                     |
| NDRIs     | Increase norepinephrine + dopamine  | Bupropion (Wellbutrin)                                           |
| Atypicals | Various                             | Mirtazapine, trazodone                                           |

:::info
Antidepressants take 2-4 weeks to begin working, and 6-8 weeks for full effect. Side effects (nausea, insomnia, sexual changes) often improve within 2 weeks.
:::

### Lifestyle Support

- **Exercise:** 30 minutes moderate activity 5x/week — as effective as medication for mild-moderate depression
- **Sleep:** Consistent bed/wake times
- **Diet:** Mediterranean diet linked to lower risk
- **Social connection:** Even 1 supportive relationship matters

## What Recovery Looks Like

- **Acute phase (6-12 weeks):** Symptoms begin to improve
- **Continuation phase (4-9 months):** Treatment continues to prevent relapse
- **Maintenance phase (1+ year):** For chronic or recurrent depression

Most people improve with treatment. About 70% respond to first antidepressant. If one doesn't work, another likely will.

## How to Support Someone with Depression

- **Listen, don't fix.** "That sounds hard" is better than "Just cheer up"
- **Offer specific help:** "I'll bring dinner Tuesday" not "Let me know if you need anything"
- **Encourage professional help** without pressuring
- **Check in regularly** — depression is isolating
- **Take suicide seriously** — always ask directly if having thoughts of self-harm

:::success
Recovery from depression is the rule, not the exception. With treatment, most people return to their usual level of functioning.
:::
