## Three Documents You'll Receive

### 1. The Bill

Sent by the hospital or doctor's office. Shows total charges. **Do not pay immediately** — wait for EOB.

### 2. Explanation of Benefits (EOB)

Sent by insurance company. Shows what they paid and what you owe. **Not a bill.** Keep for comparison.

### 3. Summary of Benefits and Coverage (SBC)

Sent with new plans. Shows what the plan covers, deductibles, copays, and out-of-pocket max.

## Parts of a Medical Bill

### Header

- Provider name and address
- Patient name and account number
- Date of service
- Medical record number

### Charges Section

| Term                    | Meaning                                  |
| ----------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| Service date            | When you received care                   |
| CPT code                | Code for procedure/test (defined by AMA) |
| Diagnosis code (ICD-10) | Code for why care was needed             |
| Charge                  | What provider billed (full price)        |
| Adjustments             | Discounts negotiated with insurance      |
| Insurance paid          | What insurance already paid              |
| Patient responsibility  | What you owe                             |

### Balance Section

Shows total charges, total adjustments, total payments, and the final amount you owe.

## Common Billing Errors

- **Duplicate charges** — billed twice for same service
- **Upcoding** — billed for a more expensive service than received (e.g., comprehensive visit vs brief check)
- **Unbundling** — services that should be a single package billed separately
- **Wrong insurance** — insurance applied incorrectly
- **Balance billing** — out-of-network provider bills difference between their charge and insurance payment (sometimes illegal)
- **Mismatched dates** — dates of service don't match your visit
- **Services you didn't receive** — wrong patient, wrong procedure

:::warning
Up to 80% of medical bills contain errors. Never assume a bill is correct.
:::

## Step-by-Step: How to Review a Bill

### Step 1: Compare to EOB

Line up the bill with your Explanation of Benefits. Everything on the bill should match — service dates, codes, provider name. If EOB says you owe $150 and the bill says $500, something is wrong.

### Step 2: Check for Known Charges

- Did you get this test/procedure?
- Was it on the right date?
- Was the provider in-network?

### Step 3: Request an Itemized Bill

If the bill shows only a total, call billing and request a detailed itemized statement. By law, you're entitled to it.

### Step 4: Ask Questions

Call the billing department. Be polite but firm:

- "Can you explain this charge?"
- "Why wasn't this covered?"
- "This CPT code doesn't match what I received."

## How to Dispute a Charge

1. **Document everything** — keep copies of bills, EOBs, insurance cards
2. **Call provider billing** first — most errors resolved at this level
3. **Call insurance** — your insurer can help if provider billing is incorrect
4. **Put it in writing** — send a written dispute via certified mail
5. **Request corrected bill** — get a revised bill before paying anything

## Financial Assistance

### Hospital Charity Care

- Non-profit hospitals must offer financial assistance by law
- Income up to 200-400% of poverty level may qualify for free or discounted care
- **You must apply** — hospitals don't automatically offer it

### Payment Plans

- Most hospitals offer interest-free payment plans
- Ask for $25-50/month, not lump sum

### Negotiating

- Offer 50-60% of the bill in cash to settle
- Hospitals often accept less than full amount
- Use phrases like: "I can't afford this. Is there a discount for paying today?"

## Red Flags

| Flag                                                            | What to Do                                   |
| --------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Bill says "past due" but you just got it                        | Dispute — premature billing                  |
| Collection agency contacts you                                  | Don't pay yet — verify the debt first        |
| Balance billing from in-network hospital                        | May violate No Surprises Act — report to CMS |
| Bill for services insurance should cover 100% (preventive care) | Call insurance — preventive care is free     |

:::info
The **No Surprises Act** (effective 2022) protects you from surprise out-of-network bills for emergency care and certain non-emergency services at in-network facilities. You can't be balance-billed for these.
:::

## When to Get Help

- **Insurance department:** File a complaint with your state
- **Patient advocate:** Many hospitals have patient advocates
- **Consumer assistance program:** Some states offer free billing help
- **Legal aid:** If facing wage garnishment or liens
- **Nonprofit credit counseling:** For large medical debts

:::success
You have rights as a patient. A correct bill is lower than the first one. Always review, question, and negotiate.
:::
