## Question 1

What is insulin resistance?

A) Cells respond poorly to insulin, requiring more insulin to control glucose
B) The pancreas makes too much insulin only in type 1 diabetes always
C) Complete absence of any insulin from birth
D) A condition cured by one day of fasting

answer: A
explanation: Resistance means reduced cellular response, driving compensatory hyperinsulinemia. Type 1 is insulin deficiency, not resistance-only; absence from birth describes type 1; one-day fasting does not cure metabolic disease.

## Question 2

Which ADA A1c range defines prediabetes?

A) 5.7% to 6.4%
B) Below 4.0%
C) Above 10% only
D) Exactly 8.0% always

answer: A
explanation: Prediabetes A1c is 5.7–6.4%; diabetes is ≥6.5%. Below 4% is unusually low; 10% suggests poorly controlled diabetes, not prediabetes definition; 8% is not the prediabetes band.

## Question 3

What did the Diabetes Prevention Program lifestyle intervention achieve?

A) Roughly 58% reduction in progression to type 2 in high-risk adults
B) 100% cure of all diabetes types
C) No effect compared to usual care
D) Only worked in people under age 12

answer: A
explanation: DPP showed major risk reduction with weight loss and activity in adults. It did not cure all diabetes, clearly beat usual care, and targeted adults at risk, not only children.

## Question 4

Which factor contributes to insulin resistance?

A) Excess visceral fat and physical inactivity
B) Wearing glasses
C) Drinking water daily
D) Seasonal pollen allergies alone

answer: A
explanation: Visceral adiposity and inactivity worsen insulin signaling. Glasses, hydration, and pollen allergies are not established drivers of insulin resistance.

## Question 5

Why screen for prediabetes even without symptoms?

A) Damage can start before symptoms and lifestyle change is most effective early
B) Prediabetes always causes immediate pain
C) Screening is only for hospitalized patients
D) There is no proven way to slow progression

answer: A
explanation: Prediabetes is often silent yet progressive; early intervention works. It is not always painful, screening is outpatient, and DPP proves progression can be slowed.
