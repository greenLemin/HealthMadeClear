## Question 1

Which nutrient deficiency is common and often linked to fatigue and anemia?

A) Iron
B) Vitamin C only
C) Sodium
D) Caffeine

answer: A
explanation: Iron deficiency reduces hemoglobin and commonly causes fatigue and anemia. Vitamin C aids absorption but alone does not cause classic anemia pattern; sodium and caffeine are not micronutrient deficiencies in this sense.

## Question 2

Who may need extra attention to vitamin B12 intake?

A) Only infants under six months
B) Older adults and people on strict vegan diets without fortification
C) Everyone should avoid B12 entirely
D) Only competitive athletes

answer: B
explanation: B12 absorption often declines with age; plant-only diets lack B12 unless fortified foods or supplements are used. Infants get B12 from formula or breast milk with adequate maternal intake; avoiding B12 is harmful; athletes are not the primary at-risk group.

## Question 3

Why test before starting high-dose supplements?

A) All deficiencies feel identical
B) Excess of some nutrients can be harmful and symptoms have many causes
C) Blood tests never detect deficiencies
D) Supplements always replace medical care

answer: B
explanation: Testing targets the right deficiency; iron overload and fat-soluble vitamin excess are dangerous. Deficiencies differ symptomatically; blood tests do detect many deficiencies; supplements complement but do not always replace treatment.

## Question 4

Which condition can impair micronutrient absorption?

A) Celiac disease affecting the small intestine
B) Wearing glasses
C) Mild seasonal allergies
D) Regular walking

answer: A
explanation: Celiac damages the small intestine and reduces absorption of iron, folate, and other nutrients. Glasses, mild allergies, and walking do not cause malabsorption syndromes.

## Question 5

Why is folic acid emphasized in pregnancy?

A) It prevents all birth defects of any kind
B) Adequate folate lowers risk of neural tube defects
C) It replaces prenatal care visits
D) It is only needed after delivery

answer: B
explanation: Folic acid before and early in pregnancy reduces neural tube defect risk — it does not prevent all birth defects, does not replace prenatal care, and is needed before conception and early pregnancy, not only postpartum.
