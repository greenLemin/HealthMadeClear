## Question 1

Urgent care is best for:

A) Chest pain and stroke
B) Same-day non-life-threatening problems
C) Annual physical exams
D) Routine prescription refills only

answer: B
explanation: Urgent care centers handle same-day problems that are not life-threatening — sprains, minor cuts, fever without severe distress, urinary symptoms. They bridge the gap when your doctor's office is closed.

## Question 2

Which belongs in the ER?

A) Mild sore throat
B) Severe trouble breathing
C) Small blister
D) Routine vaccination

answer: B
explanation: Severe trouble breathing, chest pain, stroke signs, heavy bleeding, and loss of consciousness need emergency care (911 or ER), not urgent care. Urgent care is not equipped for every emergency.

## Question 3

What does FAST stand for in stroke warning?

A) Face, Arm, Speech, Time to call 911
B) Food, Activity, Sleep, Temperature
C) Fever, Asthma, Stress, Tiredness
D) None of the above

answer: A
explanation: FAST reminds you: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call 911. Stroke treatment is time-sensitive — minutes matter. Do not drive yourself if symptoms are sudden and severe.

## Question 4

Why follow up with primary care after urgent care?

A) Not necessary
B) To coordinate ongoing treatment and records
C) Urgent care replaces your doctor forever
D) Only for children

answer: B
explanation: Your primary care doctor coordinates long-term treatment and records. After urgent care, follow up so medications, referrals, and test results stay connected to your overall care plan.

## Question 5

When unsure about severity:

A) Wait days at home
B) Consider emergency care or call 911
C) Only use online forums
D) Skip all care

answer: B
explanation: High fever with stiff neck, confusion, or rash; injury with bone visible; or symptoms rapidly worsening may need ER-level care. When unsure, call 911 — dispatchers can help you decide.
