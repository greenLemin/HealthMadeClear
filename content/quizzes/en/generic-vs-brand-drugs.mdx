## Question 1

Generics must have the same:

A) Only color
B) Active ingredient and strength
C) Marketing name
D) Price as brand

answer: B
explanation: Generic drugs must contain the same active ingredient at the same strength as the brand version. Inactive fillers may differ, but the medicine that treats your condition is equivalent for most drugs.

## Question 2

Why might insurance prefer generics?

A) They are less regulated
B) Lower cost with equivalent effectiveness for most drugs
C) They never work
D) They are always brands

answer: B
explanation: Generics cost less because manufacturers do not repeat expensive marketing and trials for the active compound. For most people they work the same — insurance often requires trying generic first.

## Question 3

After switching to generic, you should:

A) Stop all follow-up
B) Report new or worsening side effects to your care team
C) Double the dose
D) Never tell the pharmacist

answer: B
explanation: If you switch to generic and notice new side effects or feel worse, tell your care team. Rarely, sensitivity to fillers matters. Do not silently stop — report changes so they can adjust.

## Question 4

Counterfeit drugs are a risk when:

A) Using licensed pharmacies
B) Buying from unverified online sellers
C) Reading labels
D) Asking questions

answer: B
explanation: Unverified online pharmacies may sell counterfeit or improperly stored drugs. Stick to licensed pharmacies in your country. Low prices from unknown sellers are a red flag, not a bargain.

## Question 5

Bioequivalent means:

A) No active drug
B) Similar blood levels compared to brand within accepted range
C) Only for vitamins
D) Illegal in the U.S.

answer: B
explanation: Bioequivalence means the generic delivers similar blood levels to the brand within FDA-accepted limits. That is why generics are approved — not because they are weaker versions of the same drug.
