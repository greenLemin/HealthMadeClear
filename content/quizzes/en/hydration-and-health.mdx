## Question 1

Mild dehydration may cause:

A) Only hunger
B) Headache and fatigue
C) Improved athletic performance
D) Lower heart rate always

answer: B
explanation: Mild dehydration often shows up as headache, fatigue, dry mouth, or dark urine before severe illness develops. These symptoms can mimic other conditions, so consider fluid intake when they appear together.

## Question 2

Who may need fluid limits instead of more water?

A) Healthy young athletes only
B) Some people with heart or kidney disease
C) Everyone over 65
D) Only children

answer: B
explanation: People with heart failure, kidney disease, or certain medications may need fluid limits set by their doctor. Drinking extra water without guidance can be harmful for them — not everyone needs the same amount.

## Question 3

A practical hydration check for many people is:

A) Never urinate
B) Pale yellow urine and drinking when thirsty
C) Only drink at meals
D) Avoid all fluids at night

answer: B
explanation: Pale yellow urine and drinking when thirsty are practical signs of adequate hydration for most healthy adults. Clear urine all day or forcing large volumes without need are not better goals for everyone.

## Question 4

Best default drink for daily hydration:

A) Sugary soda
B) Water
C) Energy drinks only
D) Alcohol

answer: B
explanation: Water is the best default drink for daily hydration — no added sugar or calories. Sugary sodas and energy drinks add empty calories. Unsweetened options are fine, but water remains the baseline recommendation.

## Question 5

Severe dehydration requires:

A) Ignoring it
B) Urgent medical evaluation
C) Only extra coffee
D) Skipping all food forever

answer: B
explanation: Severe dehydration — confusion, fainting, no urine for many hours, rapid heartbeat — needs urgent medical care. Home remedies alone are not enough when the body shows signs of significant fluid loss.
