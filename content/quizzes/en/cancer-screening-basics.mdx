## Question 1

Main purpose of cancer screening?

A) Diagnose every symptom
B) Find early signs before symptoms
C) Replace treatment
D) Guarantee no cancer ever

answer: B
explanation: Screening tests look for early cancer or precancerous changes before you feel sick. They are not the same as diagnosing every symptom — they are preventive tools for people at recommended ages and risk levels.

## Question 2

Typical starting age for colon screening in U.S. guidelines?

A) 18
B) 45
C) 70
D) Only if symptoms

answer: B
explanation: U.S. guidelines now recommend colorectal cancer screening starting at age 45 for average-risk adults. Earlier screening may be needed with family history or certain conditions. Age 18 or 70 alone are not the standard start ages.

## Question 3

Why is family history important?

A) It never changes recommendations
B) It can lead to earlier or more frequent screening
C) Doctors already know it
D) Only matters for children

answer: B
explanation: A parent or sibling with certain cancers can raise your risk. Doctors may recommend starting mammograms, colonoscopy, or other tests earlier or repeating them more often. They cannot assume your history without you sharing it.

## Question 4

What if a screening result is abnormal?

A) Ignore it
B) Ask what it means and next steps
C) Stop all future screening
D) Self-treat

answer: B
explanation: An abnormal result does not always mean cancer, but it needs follow-up. Ask what the finding means, what test comes next, and how soon. Ignoring results or stopping all future screening is unsafe.

## Question 5

Can screening miss cancer?

A) No — it is 100% accurate
B) Yes — report new symptoms even after normal results
C) Only in young people
D) Only for skin cancer

answer: B
explanation: No screening test catches every cancer. False negatives happen. If you develop new symptoms — bleeding, lumps, persistent pain — report them even if a recent screen was normal.
